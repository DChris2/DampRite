Learn about Damp

A quick, practical guide to the most common causes of damp and how to address them safely and effectively.

History of DPC (Damp-Proof Course)

Damp-proof courses became common in UK housebuilding in the late 19th to early 20th century, evolving from slate and bitumen layers to modern chemical and membrane systems. Their purpose remains the same: stop moisture from wicking upward through capillary action.

The Three Types of Damp

1) Rising Damp

  • Signs: Tide marks, salts (white powder), crumbling skirting, paint blistering at low level.
  • Causes: Failed or bridged DPC, high external ground levels, saturated masonry.
  • Prevention/Fix: Lower ground levels, clear air bricks, repair/insert DPC, breathable plasters, controlled drying.

2) Penetrating Damp

  • Signs: Damp patches on walls/ceilings after rain, localised staining, decayed timber.
  • Causes: Leaks, cracked render, porous brick/stone, failed flashing, blocked gutters.
  • Prevention/Fix: Maintain roofs/gutters, repoint or repair render, seal penetrations, selective masonry treatment.

3) Condensation

  • Signs: Black mould, window condensation, musty smells, cold corners.
  • Causes: High humidity, cold surfaces, poor ventilation or heating patterns.
  • Prevention/Fix: Extract fans, background ventilation, consistent gentle heating, reduce moisture generation, consider dehumidification.

Weather, Ventilation & Health

Cold, wet spells increase risk. Airflow helps fabric stay dry; keep vents and air bricks clear of any vegetation preventing air from reaching the wall. Protect residents, especially children and those with respiratory conditions: use PPE when cleaning mould and avoid dry-scrubbing.
